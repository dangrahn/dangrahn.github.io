After three years of building, experimenting, and learning, we made the difficult decision to wind down Radikal.Studio. This wasn't easy, but it was right.

## What Happened

The short answer: our investments didn't work out.

We made three co-building investments with promising founders. Despite genuine effort, none bore fruit. In hindsight, we took on whatever came through our weak deal pipeline rather than being selective.

We pivoted to internal products that never found product-market fit. And to keep the lights on, we relied on consultancy work—which paid bills but pulled focus from our real mission. You can't build moonshots when you're billing hours.

## What I Learned

**You can't manufacture ideas.** We tried to be a machine for generating startups. But the best ideas come from lived experience, from personal frustration, from problems you can't stop thinking about. We ended up with ideas that looked good on paper but lacked the fire that makes founders push through hard times.

**Sweat equity is complicated.** Investing time instead of cash puts you in a strange limbo—deeply invested but not in the driver's seat. The dynamics with founders get messy when everyone's stretched thin.

**Team matters most.** Despite the outcome, I wouldn't trade this experience. We were three friends with high ambitions who cared for and challenged each other. Different skills, same drive. No egos, no blame—just figuring it out together. Thanks Masafumi Hamada and Jon Neverland.

## What's Next

This experience directly shaped how I think about validation now. Watching founders (including us) build products without truly validating core assumptions first became a pattern I couldn't ignore.

That frustration led me to start working on something new. More on that soon.

The chapter is closed. But the relationships endure. To everyone who believed in Radikal.Studio—thank you. The journey was worth it.
