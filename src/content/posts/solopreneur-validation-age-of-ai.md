AI has made it easier than ever to build a product. Agentic tools, code generation, no-code platforms. The barrier to shipping something has never been lower, and more people are becoming product builders because of it.

But the barrier to building something people actually want to pay for? That hasn't changed at all.

I'm a solopreneur. I build on the side because I want to spend more time on ideas I'm passionate about. But I always have more ideas than time, and I kept picking the ones that excited me most and jumping straight into code. No real validation. Just building and hoping.

By validation I mean two things: product-market fit (does this solve a problem people will pay for?) and distribution (can *I* actually reach them?). Both have to be true, or the product fails.

And it's not just me. After months of following how other solopreneurs build and launch in public, I keep seeing the same thing, and it's getting worse as AI lowers the building barrier further: talented builders shipping products nobody asked for, skipping validation, and wondering why nobody signs up.

It took four years of running a bootstrapped venture studio to really understand how important it is to test ideas with customers early. When you're evaluating multiple ideas with limited resources, you can't afford to build first and hope for the best.

## Why I Had to Figure This Out Myself

At my previous workplaces, I always had UX design colleagues who handled customer research. They ran the interviews, synthesized the insights, and handed me problems worth solving. I never had to build that muscle myself.

Going solo changed everything. No UX team. No co-founder to push back on my ideas. No product manager talking to customers. Just me. I could build anything, but I had no idea whether anyone would pay for it. That's the gap I had to close on my own.

## A Simple Validation Framework

I wish someone had told me this years ago: **you can test most ideas without writing a single line of code.**

Over time, I boiled it down to four steps:

**Step 1: Lean Canvas**
- Start with a one-page business canvas: map out your problem, solution, target customer, and unique value proposition
- This forces you to articulate your assumptions before testing them. If you can't clearly describe the problem and who has it, you're not ready to validate anything

**Step 2: Deep Research**
- Research the market: who are the competitors, what gaps exist, and how big is the opportunity?
- Build a picture of the landscape before you test your own idea against it

**Step 3: Signals**
- Look for demand signals: Are people actively complaining about this problem? Willing to pay for alternatives? Is the trend growing or declining?
- Assess your differentiation: do you have a real advantage over what already exists?
- If there's no evidence of demand and nothing that sets you apart from existing solutions, it's probably not worth pursuing

**Step 4: Validate**
- Identify the key hypotheses your business depends on — the assumptions that, if wrong, would kill the idea
- Create landing pages with different calls to action (waitlist, purchase, book a demo) to test varying levels of commitment
- Drive targeted visitors to them (ads, community posts, cold outreach) and measure conversion rates across variants
- Talk to real potential users using a structured interview script — questions designed to learn about their needs, frustrations, and willingness to pay without leading them toward the answer you want to hear
- If nobody converts and the conversations reveal weak interest, your positioning is wrong or the problem isn't urgent enough

No code. Just asking the market before you build.

Obviously, no validation effort gives you a guaranteed answer. But that's not the point. This framework helps me make *informed* decisions instead of blind bets. I'd rather act on imperfect data than on no data at all.

## "Just Ship an MVP Instead"

I've told myself this one too. With AI and modern tools, I can build an MVP so fast. Why bother with landing pages and customer interviews? Just ship something and see if people use it.

For the simplest ideas, maybe. But for anything with more advanced business logic, building a functional MVP still takes weeks. Weeks of evenings and weekends before I've learned anything about whether people will pay. A few days of validation upfront can save me from that. I wish I'd accepted that sooner.

## "But I'm Just Copying a Working Business"

I've seen this advice a lot: take a proven idea and build your own version. It sounds like a shortcut. Someone else already validated the concept, so you just have to build it better, right? But validation isn't only about the product. It's also about distribution. Can *you* reach the right customers? A proven product with no way to get it into people's hands is still a failed project. Even for cloned ideas, early validation is just as important.
